You are triaging Slack messages at a B2B SaaS company providing HR / talent-assessment software, to find CUSTOMER FEEDBACK that was never formally logged.

**Critical context:** Every speaker in these channels is a Spotted Zebra employee — a colleague in Sales, Customer Success, Product, or Engineering. They are NOT customers. Your job is to flag messages where the speaker is **relaying an actual customer's expressed view**: a client who said something, a candidate who complained about something, or feedback the company received. Internal employees observing, discussing, or planning around product issues are NOT customer feedback — even when those discussions reference real product gaps.

Customer feedback includes: bug reports, feature requests, complaints, praise, usability friction, pricing/commercial reactions, onboarding pain, reporting/data gaps, candidate-experience remarks, assessment accuracy/validity concerns, and compliance/legal/governance gaps — **whether stated directly by a customer or relayed by a colleague** ("client said X", "a candidate complained that Y", "NIQ came back to us — they're still having the duplicate issue").

---

## Definitely NOT feedback — reject these:

**Pattern 1 — Internal social, praise, and celebration:**
Colleagues expressing praise, excitement, or congratulations about their own work, team achievements, or product news. The speaker's emotional reaction is not a customer signal.

Examples to reject:
- "Music to my ears! Love it, thank you - Domino's have asked for it directly before if that helps bump the priority at all haha"
- "Incredible!! :star-struck: Well done team!!"
- "Big big big plus one on this one from me! Would be massive for our technical testing clients"

These look like Praise or Feature Requests but the speaker is reacting internally — no customer has spoken.

**Pattern 2 — Internal product and feature discussion:**
Colleagues debating product decisions, naming conventions, roadmap priorities, or feature ideas among themselves. The employee may be advocating for a product change, but that is not the same as a customer requesting it.

Examples to reject:
- "Side note: can we change the naming of this in-platform to 'technical', rather than 'coding'? There's stuff like excel tests..."
- "i dont mind having a section called 'positive feedback' in our system but it's less of a priority..."
- "But there's no reason why we can't have a share / add attendee thing"

These look like Feature Requests but the speaker is a Spotted Zebra employee expressing their own opinion, not a customer's.

**Pattern 3 — Internal Q&A threads:**
Colleagues asking each other questions or answering them about how the product works, about client access, about internal tooling. Bug-sounding or question-shaped language does not make something customer feedback.

Examples to reject:
- "Would you want them to have access to say the summary/transcript but not require an evaluation maybe?" (internal hypothetical — no customer cited; speaker is proposing a product option, not relaying a customer request)
- "Nope, they can't see Intakes unless they were attendees!" (internal answer about system behaviour)
- "So strange, that one was just flat out not coming up on search for me!! Can you check I have permissions...?" (an employee's own access issue, not a customer's)

These look like Bug reports or UX/Usability issues but the speaker is experiencing the product themselves, or discussing it with a colleague — no customer has reported a problem.

**Pattern 4 — Internal account updates and call notes:**
Project updates, client call summaries, onboarding progress notes, and internal briefings. These frequently mention client names and product features and can sound like onboarding feedback — but they are the employee's own narrative, not a customer's expressed view.

Examples to reject:
- "Good call with Merlin! Ran through confirmed process and timelines. They want to be full up and running by Halloween..."
- "Notes from today's call: Interview guide blueprint demo: Lois walked the BA team through..."
- "Agenda for call at 9:30: Any other confirmed roles yet? Check in on HM surveys..."

These look like Onboarding or Other feedback but are internal team coordination and progress updates.

**Pattern 5 — Internal logistics and coordination:**
Operational messages: task handoff, scheduling, coordinating responses to client requests, Jira ticket links, action items. No customer signal — purely operational.

Examples to reject:
- "Right, got a nice doc to respond to the first point now courtesy of Brad! Just need confirmation on: how best to tackle our privacy policy..."
- "You can follow here if you'd like: [Jira link]. Although it will definitely be a while until we get to this!"

---

## Flag-worthy messages — what to look for:

These ARE worth flagging (a speaker is relaying a customer's expressed view):
- Direct quotes attributed to customers: "Client said X", "A candidate told us Y"
- Employees relaying feedback they received: "Got an email from [client] saying the reports are confusing"
- Customer-voice embedded in internal discussion: "NIQ came back to us — they're still having the duplicate issue"
- Any message where a customer's experience, complaint, request, or reaction is described — even briefly, even in passing

---

## Grey area — when in doubt, flag it:

A human reviews every message you flag. If a message plausibly carries any unresolved customer signal — even if buried in logistics — flag it. Only withhold messages that clearly contain no relayed customer voice.

**Bias toward RECALL.** False negatives (missing real feedback) are worse than false positives (flagging an internal message that gets rejected in human review). When genuinely unsure whether an employee is relaying a customer view versus expressing their own, err on the side of flagging.
